The Artist

The painter and her room.

Sophia Wilde was born in London in 1989 and trained at the Slade School of Fine Art (BA, 2011), followed by a residency at Carpintarias de Sao Lazaro in Lisbon (2013). For the first decade of her practice she painted figuratively, mostly portraits, mostly outdoors. The shift to interiors came after her father's death in 2022, when she returned to his Hampstead flat to clear it out and ended up not clearing it at all. She painted it instead.

What she found, over the months that followed, was that interior space concentrates light differently than open air. A painting of a room is, at heart, a painting of how light meets walls, ledges, edges. Wilde's work since 2023 has been almost entirely in this register: domestic thresholds, window ledges, the sides of doors. The room is the subject. The light is the verb.

She works slowly. Most paintings take six to ten weeks. She returns to the same room for the same hour of the same day across multiple sittings. The result is a body of work that is dense, intimate, and unfashionably patient. Her first solo show, Interior Light at Blonde Contemporary in February 2024, sold out on opening night. Her second, North Window, runs at the same gallery in October 2026.

The Lineage

Where the work sits.

Wilde's interior light studies sit in a long, quiet lineage. The first painter most commonly invoked is Johannes Vermeer (1632 to 1675), whose Dutch Golden Age domestic interiors made north light a subject in its own right. The second is Vilhelm Hammershoi (1864 to 1916), the Danish painter whose grey, almost depopulated rooms in central Copenhagen extended Vermeer's project into modernity. Both are touchstones the artist herself names.

Closer to the present, Wilde shares territory with painters like Mamma Andersson (Swedish, b. 1962) and looser parallels with the muted melancholy of Luc Tuymans (Belgian, b. 1958). Her own work is more figurative than either. Where Tuymans erases, Wilde holds on.

What contemporary realism means here.

Contemporary realism is not a movement with a manifesto. It is a loose grouping of mostly figurative painters working from observation, often slowly, often interested in the everyday. The label distinguishes them from the previous generation's conceptual and pop registers, and from the more emotionally charged figurative painting of the late 20th century. The values shared across the grouping are roughly: paint as a medium, observation as a method, and the room as a serious subject.

Wilde's work belongs to this tradition without programmatically defending it. She is less interested in arguing for realism than in just doing it.

Studio & Process

How a Wilde painting is made.

  • Site visits. Wilde paints from observation. She rarely uses photography. A typical painting begins with two to three weeks of returning to the same room at the same time of day to make graphite studies.
  • Linen, primed by the artist. She works on Belgian linen primed with a warm oil ground. Stretchers are built in the studio.
  • Underpainting. A monochrome umber underpainting maps tonal masses before colour. This stage often takes a week.
  • Glazes, slow. Colour is built in thin glazes over four to six weeks. Wilde works only in natural light. Cloudy days are working days.
  • Signed verso. Title, date, and signature are written on the back of the linen, never the front.
